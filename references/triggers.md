## 1. Loss Aversion

**Mechanism:** Losses feel ~2x as painful as equivalent gains feel good (Kahneman & Tversky, Prospect Theory). The brain works harder to avoid losing than to gain.

**Use when:** The buyer doesn't feel urgency; the product prevents a problem; competitors sell only benefits.

**Examples:**
- ❌ "Save $500 with our tool" → ✅ "You're losing $500 every month you don't switch"
- ❌ "Get more customers" → ✅ "Your competitor is taking the customers you never called back"

**Caution:** Pure fear paralyzes. Always pair the loss with an immediate, easy escape route (the CTA).

## 2. Social Proof

**Mechanism:** Under uncertainty, the brain copies the herd — it's a survival shortcut. The more similar the herd is to the buyer, the stronger the effect.

**Use when:** Cold audiences, trust deficit, new/unknown product.

**Forms, ranked by power:**
1. Specific results from similar people ("A Dhaka boutique owner doubled orders in 6 weeks")
2. Numbers in motion ("2,300 downloads this month" beats "10,000 total")
3. Named testimonials with faces > anonymous quotes
4. Logos / "as seen in"

**Caution:** Negative social proof backfires: "Most people don't back up their data" normalizes NOT acting. Always show the desired behavior as the norm.

## 3. Anchoring

**Mechanism:** The first number seen becomes the reference point; everything after is judged relative to it — even when the anchor is irrelevant.

**Use when:** Price objections, premium positioning, discount framing.

**Examples:**
- Show the ৳12,000 package before the ৳4,000 one — the ৳4,000 now feels cheap
- "Agencies charge $2,000 for this audit. The guide is $29."
- Cross out the original price; never show the discount price alone

## 4. Scarcity & Urgency

**Mechanism:** Scarce = valuable (evolutionary resource competition). A closing window forces System 1 to decide now rather than defer.

**Use when:** The buyer believes but delays ("I'll do it later" is the biggest killer of warm conversions).

**Forms:**
- Quantity: "50 seats, 43 taken" (specific numbers beat "limited spots")
- Time: real deadlines with a reason ("Price rises when the July cohort opens")
- Access: "Members only" / application required

**Caution:** MUST be real. Fake countdown timers that reset destroy trust permanently. If nothing is genuinely scarce, create a real bonus deadline instead.

## 5. Framing

**Mechanism:** The same fact, framed differently, produces different decisions. "90% fat-free" outsells "10% fat" — identical information.

**Reframes that sell:**
- Price → per-day: "৳3,000/year" → "৳8 a day — less than your tea"
- Cost → investment with return: "Pays for itself after your first client"
- Feature → consequence: "8GB RAM" → "Never see a loading spinner again"

## 6. Reciprocity

**Mechanism:** Receiving something creates a psychological debt the brain wants to settle (Cialdini). Free value first → buyer feels compelled to give back attention, trust, then money.

**Use when:** Cold audiences, lead generation, DM funnels.

**Example:** Give a genuinely useful free guide → the ask ("follow", "comment", eventually "buy") feels natural, not demanded. The free thing must have standalone value — a thin "freebie" creates no debt.

## 7. Authority

**Mechanism:** The brain defers to perceived expertise to save cognitive effort (white-coat effect).

**Forms:** Credentials, published results, specific numbers only an insider would know, borrowed authority ("CPA-designed", "used at Google"), demonstration (teach something real — authority through competence beats authority through claims).

## 8. Decoy Effect

**Mechanism:** An asymmetrically-dominated third option shifts choice toward the target. Buyers can't judge absolute value, only relative.

**Use when:** Pricing pages, packages.

**Example:**
- Basic ৳2,000 / Pro ৳5,000 → most pick Basic
- Basic ৳2,000 / **Plus ৳4,500 (fewer features than Pro)** / Pro ৳5,000 → most pick Pro

The decoy exists to be rejected — it makes the target look obviously superior.

## 9. Paradox of Choice

**Mechanism:** More options → more anxiety → fewer decisions (Iyengar's jam study: 24 flavors got 3% conversion, 6 flavors got 30%).

**Rules:** One CTA per page/post. Max 3 pricing tiers. One offer per campaign. If the user's copy asks for two actions, cut one.

## 10. Endowment Effect

**Mechanism:** Owning something (even briefly or imaginarily) raises its value in the buyer's mind.

**Forms:** Free trials, "your dashboard is ready", ownership language before purchase ("Your plan includes…"), future pacing ("Imagine opening your laptop Monday and the report is already done").

## 11. Commitment & Consistency

**Mechanism:** After a small public "yes," the brain pressures itself to stay consistent with that identity.

**Use when:** Long funnels, comment-to-DM strategies.

**Example:** Comment a keyword (micro-yes) → accept DM (second yes) → download (third) → buy (consistent conclusion). Each step is tiny; the chain is powerful.

## 12. Zeigarnik Effect (Open Loops)

**Mechanism:** The brain cannot let go of incomplete stories — unresolved tension keeps attention captive.

**Use when:** Hooks, sequences, retention.

**Examples:** "I lost my biggest client last year. Best thing that ever happened." (must read on) · "The third mistake is the one that cost me the most — I'll get to it."

## 13. Von Restorff Effect (Contrast)

**Mechanism:** The item that differs from its surroundings gets remembered. In a feed of same-looking posts, different wins over better.

**Use when:** Ad creative, feed content, pricing highlight ("Most popular" badge on one tier only).

## 14. Peak-End Rule

**Mechanism:** Experiences are judged by their peak moment and their ending — not the average.

**Use when:** Structuring content, onboarding, offers. End every post/email/page on the strongest note (the promise, the transformation), never on logistics. Put one "wow" moment in every deliverable.

## 15. Cognitive Fluency

**Mechanism:** Easy-to-process = trustworthy and true, to the brain. Hard-to-read fonts, jargon, and long sentences literally reduce believability.

**Rules:** Short sentences. Concrete words. One idea per line. If a clever phrase needs a second read, kill it.

## 16. Specificity

**Mechanism:** Specific claims feel observed; round numbers feel invented. "Increased revenue 34% in 11 weeks" beats "significantly grow your revenue."

**Rule:** Replace every abstraction with a number, name, or timeframe. "Many customers" → "412 customers". "Fast" → "under 40 seconds".

## 17. Identity & Self-Signaling

**Mechanism:** People buy what confirms who they believe they are (or want to be). Identity beats utility.

**Examples:** "For founders who refuse to outsource their thinking" · "Serious sellers track every taka." The buyer joins a tribe, not a transaction.

## 18. Fresh Start Effect

**Mechanism:** Temporal landmarks (Monday, month start, New Year, birthdays, Eid) make people dramatically more likely to start new behaviors.

**Use when:** Timing campaigns and CTAs — "Start this Sunday" outperforms "start anytime."

## 19. Charm & Prestige Pricing

**Mechanism:** ৳999 is processed as "nine hundred something" (left-digit effect) — right for value positioning. Round ৳1,000 signals quality — right for premium positioning. Removing currency symbols reduces payment pain.

## 20. Negativity Bias

**Mechanism:** Negative information grabs and holds attention harder than positive (survival wiring). Mistake-framed content outperforms tip-framed content.

**Example:** "5 tips for better ads" → weak. "The ad mistake burning 40% of your budget" → strong.

**Caution:** Open negative, close positive — problem → solution, always. Never leave the reader in the pit.
