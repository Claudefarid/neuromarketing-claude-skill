# Neuromarketing

Turn marketing copy into brain-compatible copy. ~95% of purchase decisions happen subconsciously (Harvard, Gerald Zaltman) — the buyer's System 1 (fast, emotional) decides, then System 2 (slow, rational) justifies. Copy that argues with System 2 loses to copy that speaks to System 1.

## Workflow

Follow these 4 steps for every copy task:

### Step 1 — Diagnose before writing

Identify, from the user's input or by asking:
- **Who buys** — and what do they *fear losing*, not just what they want to gain
- **Buying temperature** — cold (never heard of you), warm (knows you), hot (comparing options)
- **The real objection** — price is rarely the true objection; it's usually risk, trust, or "will this work for ME?"

### Step 2 — Select 2–4 triggers (never all)

Stacking every trigger reads as manipulation and triggers reactance (the brain resists when it senses it's being pushed). Pick the 2–4 that match the diagnosis:

| Buyer situation | Lead triggers |
|---|---|
| Doesn't feel the problem | Loss aversion, vivid pain framing |
| Feels problem, doubts solution | Social proof, authority, specificity |
| Believes solution, delays action | Scarcity, fresh-start, urgency |
| Comparing you vs competitor | Anchoring, decoy, contrast (Von Restorff) |
| Price objection | Anchoring, reframing (cost-per-day), decoy pricing |
| Trust deficit (cold audience) | Reciprocity, risk reversal, micro-commitment |

Full trigger library with mechanisms and examples: read [references/triggers.md](references/triggers.md). For tone calibration, see before/after rewrites in [references/examples.md](references/examples.md).

### Step 3 — Apply to the format

Each channel has its own psychology (attention span, intent level, layout). Read [references/applications.md](references/applications.md) for the channel playbook:
- Ad copy & hooks · Landing pages · Pricing pages · Offers & promos · Email/DM · Social posts

### Step 4 — Audit pass

Before delivering, check the copy against these:

- [ ] First line creates tension or curiosity within ~1.7 seconds of reading
- [ ] Speaks to one reader ("you"), not an audience ("everyone", "people")
- [ ] Concrete and specific — numbers, names, timeframes (brain trusts specifics, skips abstractions)
- [ ] At least one loss-framed line, not only gain-framed
- [ ] Proof element present (number, testimonial, authority, demonstration)
- [ ] One clear CTA — a confused brain does nothing (paradox of choice)
- [ ] Reads effortlessly — cognitive fluency: simple beats clever; a 12-year-old should follow it
- [ ] Every claim is TRUE (see Ethics below)

## Output format

When writing new copy, deliver:
1. The copy itself
2. A short "**Triggers used:**" list naming each trigger and where it appears — so the user learns the pattern, not just receives text

When auditing existing copy, deliver:
1. What's killing conversion (ranked, worst first, each tied to a named principle)
2. Rewritten version
3. Before/after explanation per change

## Language

Match the user's market language. Triggers are universal — loss aversion works identically in Bangla, Banglish, or English. When writing for the Bangladeshi market, keep emotional lines in Bangla and technical/brand terms in English (Banglish), e.g.:

> ❌ "আমাদের কোর্সে অনেক ভালো content আছে" (feature, abstract)
> ✅ "আপনার competitor গত মাসে এটা শিখে ফেলেছে। আপনি এখনো শুরু করেননি।" (loss aversion + social proof + specificity)

## Ethics — non-negotiable

Neuromarketing amplifies truth; it must never manufacture lies:
- Never invent fake scarcity ("only 3 left" when stock is unlimited), fake testimonials, or fake statistics
- Never use these techniques for products that harm the buyer
- If the user asks for a fabricated claim, offer a truthful alternative that uses the same trigger (e.g., real deadline instead of fake stock count)

Fake triggers convert once and destroy the brand permanently — the brain remembers betrayal (negativity bias) far longer than satisfaction.
